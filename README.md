# AI Summer Course for Financial Professionals
## AI Literacy, Governance, and Institutional Implementation in Finance

This repository hosts the public GitHub landing page and resource map for the **AI Summer Course for Financial Professionals**. The course is designed for industry leaders, corporate leaders, startup leaders, board members, senior executives, financial professionals, and advanced students who need to understand artificial intelligence as a serious institutional capability.

The Summer Course expands the earlier Easter Course into a broader and more complete preparation for the institutional implementation of AI in finance. It is not a course about using chatbots. It is a course about understanding AI, governing AI, and implementing AI across real financial domains.

The central principle of the course is:

> **AI must be understood, then governed, and only then implemented at institutional scale.**

Artificial intelligence is one of the largest technological revolutions of our time. In finance, however, its value will not be captured by casual experimentation alone. It will be captured by institutions and leaders capable of transforming AI from a promising technology into a governed industrial process: auditable, reproducible, supervised, strategically aligned, and connected to real business domains.

## Course Purpose

The course is designed to give participants the intellectual, technical, governance, and applied foundations required to participate meaningfully in AI implementation at the institutional level.

Financial institutions are not simply adopting new software. They are redesigning how judgment, analysis, documentation, risk evaluation, client interaction, compliance, investment processes, reporting, and strategic decision-making can be supported by increasingly capable AI systems.

For that reason, the course does not treat AI as a narrow technical topic or as a matter of chatbot usage. It treats AI as a new layer of institutional infrastructure.

Participants move through three stages:

1. **Understand AI deeply enough to interpret it, question it, and use it with confidence.**
2. **Recognize AI as an industrial process that requires governance, controls, accountability, and operating models.**
3. **Apply AI domain by domain within the financial sector, translating concepts into practical institutional workflows.**

The course is built for people who must make decisions about AI, supervise AI initiatives, evaluate vendors, participate in implementation committees, understand technical proposals, assess risk, and identify where real institutional value can be created.

---

## The Three-Pillar Architecture

The Summer Course is organized around three pillars. Each pillar corresponds to a different level of institutional preparation.

| Pillar | Core Question | Main Objective | Institutional Role |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Pillar 1** | What is AI and how does it work? | Build conceptual and technical literacy. | Understand AI rather than merely use it. |
| **Pillar 2** | How should AI be governed as an industrial process? | Build governance maturity and operating discipline. | Convert AI from experimentation into controlled institutional capability. |
| **Pillar 3** | How is AI implemented across financial domains? | Translate AI into practical financial workflows. | Deploy AI in domain-specific, auditable, supervised use cases. |

The three pillars are sequential but mutually reinforcing. Pillar 1 gives participants the language and conceptual foundations. Pillar 2 teaches them how to govern AI inside institutions. Pillar 3 shows how the same ideas become concrete in investment analysis, risk, credit, compliance, capital markets, asset management, disclosure, due diligence, and other financial domains.

# Pillar 1. The Absolute Basics
## Understanding AI, Not Just Using It

Pillar 1 provides the essential foundation. Its purpose is to give participants the context required to understand AI, not simply consume AI products.

This pillar is not designed to turn participants into machine learning researchers. It is designed to give them enough conceptual and technical independence to read, interpret, challenge, and supervise AI-related work.

Participants learn the basic mechanics of modern AI: data, representation, embeddings, tokens, model families, neural networks, training, inference, evaluation, prompt design, structured outputs, generative AI, transformers, reasoning models, multimodality, and agents.

The central outcome of Pillar 1 is **AI literacy for institutional judgment**.

---

## Pillar 1 Learning Objectives

By the end of Pillar 1, participants should be able to:

1. Explain what AI is in institutional and technical language.
2. Distinguish AI, machine learning, deep learning, generative AI, and agentic AI.
3. Understand the basic roles of data, features, embeddings, tokens, parameters, and models.
4. Explain training and inference without relying on superficial metaphors.
5. Understand why AI systems can be powerful and still unreliable.
6. Distinguish fluency from truth, confidence from evidence, and prediction from reasoning.
7. Read AI proposals, vendor presentations, and technical summaries with greater independence.
8. Recognize basic failure modes such as hallucination, overfitting, bias, leakage, fragility, and lack of grounding.
9. Understand why governance cannot be added at the end; it must be designed into AI usage from the beginning.

# Pillar 2. AI as an Industrial Process
## Governance, Control, and Corporate Implementation

Pillar 2 reframes AI as a corporate and institutional production system.

AI is not about chatbots. In serious financial environments, AI becomes part of business processes, analytical workflows, documentation chains, client-facing systems, model risk structures, compliance reviews, audit trails, and governance frameworks.

This pillar helps participants understand AI implementation as an industrial process that must be designed, supervised, documented, and controlled.

The central outcome of Pillar 2 is **governance maturity for corporate AI implementation**.

---

## Pillar 2 Learning Objectives

By the end of Pillar 2, participants should be able to:

1. Explain why AI implementation is an operating model challenge, not merely a software adoption challenge.
2. Distinguish experimentation, controlled pilots, reusable assets, and enterprise-grade AI systems.
3. Design AI workflows with evidence boundaries, verification status, human review, and escalation gates.
4. Understand prompt contracts, schema-first outputs, deterministic gates, and fail-closed generation.
5. Identify AI-related risks across model risk, operational risk, compliance risk, reputational risk, and strategic risk.
6. Understand the role of auditability, reproducibility, traceability, and documentation.
7. Evaluate vendor proposals and internal AI initiatives from a governance perspective.
8. Understand the role of AI committees, decision rights, policies, standards, and institutional accountability.
9. Recognize why the most valuable AI systems are not isolated prompts but governed workflows embedded in institutional processes.

# Pillar 3. Domain-by-Domain Implementation in Finance
## Hands-On AI Implementation Across the Financial Sector

Pillar 3 moves from understanding and governance into practical implementation across financial domains.

This pillar is extensive because financial institutions are not single-process organizations. AI implementation must be adapted to the specific logic of each domain: investment analysis, portfolio construction, credit, risk, compliance, disclosure, due diligence, capital markets, real estate, client advisory, operations, and strategic decision support.

The central outcome of Pillar 3 is **domain-specific AI implementation capability**.

Pillar 3 asks a practical question:

> How can AI be converted into governed, auditable, useful workflows in each major financial domain?

---

## Pillar 3 Learning Objectives

By the end of Pillar 3, participants should be able to:

1. Translate AI capabilities into specific financial workflows.
2. Identify which financial tasks are suitable for AI support and which require stricter human control.
3. Design AI workflows for investment research, credit, risk, compliance, disclosure, due diligence, and portfolio analysis.
4. Use AI to structure, summarize, compare, analyze, and challenge financial information.
5. Build domain-specific notebooks and prototypes that demonstrate the mechanics of implementation.
6. Apply governance controls to each domain rather than treating governance as a generic appendix.
7. Distinguish productivity automation from accountable professional decision support.
8. Create auditable outputs that can be reviewed by supervisors, committees, clients, regulators, or internal control functions.
9. Understand how domain expertise remains essential even when AI systems become more capable.

# Recommended Learning Sequence

Participants are encouraged to move through the course in the following order.

## Stage 1. Orientation

Begin with the course landing page, the main deck, and the course handbook. The objective is to understand the full architecture before going into individual topics.

## Stage 2. Pillar 1 Foundations

Work through the absolute basics. The goal is not to memorize technical terminology, but to build enough conceptual confidence to understand what AI systems are doing and why they require supervision.

## Stage 3. Pillar 2 Governance and Industrialization

Move from AI literacy into institutional governance. Participants should learn to think in terms of operating models, controls, escalation, auditability, reproducibility, and accountable deployment.

## Stage 4. Pillar 3 Domain Implementation

Select the financial domains most relevant to the participant's role. A portfolio manager may focus on investment analysis, portfolio construction, and risk. A credit professional may focus on credit memos, covenant review, and borrower monitoring. A compliance professional may focus on regulatory monitoring, policy review, and escalation workflows. A senior executive may focus on strategy, operating models, AI committees, and institutional transformation.

## Stage 5. Capstone Integration

The final stage is to connect all three pillars into a single institutional implementation view: AI literacy, governance architecture, and domain-specific deployment.

# Governance-First Methodology

The course is built around the idea that artificial intelligence in finance must be governed from the beginning.

Key governance themes include:

- Evidence boundaries.
- Verification status.
- Human review.
- Prompt contracts.
- Schema-first outputs.
- Deterministic gates.
- Audit bundles.
- Reproducibility.
- Escalation pathways.
- Model risk awareness.
- Refusal logic.
- Separation of facts, assumptions, open questions, and analysis.
- Accountability for professional judgment.

AI is treated as a professional support capability, not as a replacement for institutional responsibility.

4. Evaluate AI tools, proposals, and workflows from a governance perspective.
5. Design basic governed AI workflows with structured outputs and review gates.
6. Identify AI risks in financial contexts.
7. Translate AI capabilities into specific financial-domain use cases.
8. Understand where AI can support financial judgment and where human accountability remains essential.
9. Participate in AI implementation committees and institutional AI strategy discussions.
10. Build or supervise applied AI notebooks, demonstrations, and prototypes.
11. Reap the potential of AI while respecting evidence, governance, risk, and professional responsibility.
